Fitbit Anti-Cheating Feature

Summary

For this project we wanted to find out why people cheat and develop an efficient way to stop it. We focused on encouraging users to use the product in a more productive and consistent way through social accountability. After researching the problems, we scoped our focus down to modifying Fitbit’s app by creating a community and incentive based solution to finalize our idea. Our process involved primary research, secondary research, and qualitative usability testing, which led us to our final solution.

My Role

My project partner, Michael, and I split the work equally. We collaborated on secondary research by focusing on different areas, then I conducted user interviews. Then we compiled our research through affinity diagramming and each creating a persona. We then developed paper prototypes on which Michael conducted usability testing. Finally, Michael compiled the documentation, which I added to and proofread.

001. Research

Secondary Research

“If there are loopholes, players find them and exploit them.” – Mike Tinney, CEO of Fitness Interactive Experience

I began by looking for user problems in the review section for the Pinterest Android app on the Google Play store. This served as a good starting point because many users contributed detailed explanations of what delighted them and what frustrated them. These four reviews stood out because they pointed out specific problems and why they were frustrated.

We began with secondary research, searching online data on why and how people cheat with their Fitbits. The reasons varied by purpose, from working professionals cheating health insurance to gain incentives and discounts to casual users having a loss of motivation. We gained many important insights from our research. For example, insurance programs were confusing to users – they thought they would have to pay higher premiums if they didn’t meet activity markers, though this was not the case.

From there, we looked at a few different areas – why Fitbits are good, why are they bad, and the psychology behind why people cheat. This is where we started to scope down our idea.

Our full secondary research can be found here.

Primary Research

Our research provided us with a basis for generating interview questions. We started with a mass list of questions created to supplement what we had found so far. As interviews were conducted, most of these questions adapted – they would change due to the user, what we had learned, and sometimes weren’t asked at all.

One of our intentions while conducting interviews was to gain perspectives beyond just current Fitbit users. We interviewed six different users, including those who used fitness tracking apps, people who had stopped using fitness trackers, and people currently using fitness trackers of different models.

“You can cheat a Fitbit, but you can’t cheat yourself.” – Zachary Sandberg

The interviews provided us with a great deal of information. One of our key insights was that people were motivated to work out less by their fitness trackers but more, in general, by friends who they worked out with. Having someone to keep them accountable provided encouragement and increased inclination to stay fit.

002. Affinity Diagramming

Pictures coming soon

Once we completed interviews, we aggregated all of the information we had found through affinity diagrams. This allowed to group information we had found in both our primary and secondary research and find common themes. The information was sorted into solutions, why people cheat, how people cheat, why people don’t use their trackers, and motivations to exercise.

We also created tried a similar strategy to brainstorm ideas for our persona. This helped us come up with goals/motivations and pain points.

003. Personas

Personas coming soon

We created two personas to represent our users. The first, Craig, is a middle-aged CPA who tracks his fitness because his work offers insurance incentives. The second persona is Amanda, a student who bought a fitness tracker to help her keep her New Year’s resolution to lose weight. We decided on creating two broadly different users to remind us that our solution needed to be be widely applicable and useful for a variety of users.

004. Prototyping

Pictures coming soon

Our next phase was to start prototyping the ideas. We started with ideas that included fit tips, local group sections, and an updated reaction feature. Our base idea was to build sketches that would represent the app’s pages and how they would link to each other. We felt that focusing our changes around our app would allow for easy adaptations during our user testing. Also, the Fitbit app is an existing platform that many users are already familiar with.

Our guiding idea during prototyping was to create features to help users meet other users and exercise together. We decided to pursue this because of the insights from our interviews on how users tend to exercise more often when they have groups. Group psychology can be a very powerful motivator.

005. Usability Testing

Pictures coming soon

During usability testing, we were able find where the design failed to communicate and where it really missed the points we were looking to improve. One thing that was brought up was that our solution seemed like a temporary fix to larger problem at hand.

Additionally, we found some flaws regarding our original prototype. We found that we were missing slides for features when mapping out the app. Secondly, we found out that with the addition of so many new features, navigating between them was a hassle and caused unnecessary friction in the flow.

We mapped out our flow to help understand where the interface had become complicated and how we could improve it.

006. Results

Our first testing revealed that there were missing screens and an overwhelming amount of features. There were also too many scenarios to feasibly test effectively in the time constraints. This led us to cut the tips, upgraded reactions, and local routes features in order to streamline the functionality and purpose of the design.

Our second testing showed that the design was easier to navigate. The new testing also showed us that the steps to get from page to page seemed more intuitive than our initial design.

Conclusion

By focusing on the social psychology aspect of motivation, we were able to create a good starting point with our prototype. In the future, to further develop this idea, we would like to conduct more research on group psychology to understand what makes them powerful and motivating. We would also simplify our prototype to create more focused features to accomplish our goal, instead of spreading it out. Fewer features could more effectively and more easily accomplish the same goal.
